🌍 Rhythm Library

All named rhythms in Rhythm list — patterns, origins, and ethnomusicological notes.

Coypu ships with a library of named rhythms drawn from global percussion traditions. Each one is accessed by sending asRhythm to a Pharo symbol, or by sending the symbol directly to to: as a shorthand. The source code of each method includes detailed ethnomusicological comments — inspect Rhythm list in a Pharo image to read them.

💡 In the step visualisers below, filled squares are triggers and empty squares are rests. Groups of 4 steps correspond to one beat in 4/4 time.

Afro-Caribbean

#rumba Cuba

The son clave in rumba form — a 16-step pattern rooted in the secular Afro-Cuban tradition of rumba drumming. Originated in late 19th-century urban Havana and Matanzas, combining African (Abakuá, yuka) and Spanish elements. The foundational rhythm of Cuban popular music.

#rumba asRhythm to: #conga.
#cumbiaClave Colombia

The clave pattern of Colombian cumbia — a three-beat cross-rhythm over 16 steps. Cumbia fuses Indigenous, African, and Spanish elements and is the foundational rhythm of Colombia's Caribbean coast. The clave is the rhythmic skeleton all other parts lock around.

#cumbiaClave asRhythm to: #conga.
#plena Puerto Rico

The plena rhythm from Puerto Rico — an Afro-Caribbean musical form that emerged in the early 20th century in coastal towns like Ponce. The pandereta (frame drum) pattern is syncopated and call-and-response in nature, reflecting its oral-history storytelling function.

#plena to: #conga.
#guaguanco Cuba

The clave pattern for guaguancó, a subgenre of Cuban rumba. More syncopated than the son clave, with characteristic anticipation on beat 4. Guaguancó is a dance form tied to competitive courting rituals (vacunao) performed in Afro-Cuban communities.

#guaguanco asRhythm to: #conga.
#bossaNova Brazil

The rhythmic DNA of bossa nova — the style developed in late-1950s Rio de Janeiro by João Gilberto and Tom Jobim. A relaxed, syncopated samba derivative played on guitar with a calm, intimate feel. The pattern here reflects the characteristic guitar-strum rhythm.

#bossaNova asRhythm to: #snare.
#tresillo Cuba / Pan-African diaspora

Three pulses in 8 steps — one of the most widespread rhythmic cells in the African diaspora. The tresillo underlies Cuban son, New Orleans second-line, reggae, and many other genres. Godfried Toussaint identified it as a Euclidean rhythm E(3,8).

#tresillo asRhythm to: #kick.

Latin & South American

#banda Mexico

The bass drum and snare pattern of banda sinaloense — the brass-heavy Mexican genre from Sinaloa state. Known for its powerful two-beat feel and close relationship with polka and corrido traditions. A staple of Northern Mexican dance music.

#banda to: #kick.
#cumbia Colombia

The basic cumbia groove — a caja (drum) pattern from the Caribbean coast of Colombia. Cumbia blends Indigenous flutes, African percussion and Spanish harmony. It spread across Latin America in the 20th century, spawning dozens of regional variants from Argentina to Mexico.

#cumbia asRhythm to: #conga.
#samba Brazil

The surdo (bass drum) and caixa (snare) pattern from Brazilian samba. Emerged in early 20th-century Rio de Janeiro with Afro-Brazilian communities from Bahia. The driving force behind Carnaval, samba schools, and countless derivative styles worldwide.

#samba asRhythm to: #snare.
🌙

Middle East & North Africa

#baladi Egypt

An Egyptian folk rhythm used in baladi (folk/classical) music and dance. Typically played on the tabla (goblet drum), it features a characteristic doum-tek-tek pattern across 8 steps. Central to Egyptian sha'bi and belly-dance music traditions.

#baladi asRhythm to: #tabla.
#samai Arab world / Turkey

A 10/8 meter (or its 10-step equivalent) common in Arabic and Ottoman classical music. The samai form is a composed instrumental genre with fixed movements. The rhythmic cycle sama'i thaqil groups as 3+2+2+3 — producing a characteristic lilt.

#samai asRhythm to: #tabla.
#karsilama Turkey / Balkans

A 9/8 Anatolian folk dance rhythm grouped as 2+2+2+3. The karşılama is a face-to-face couple dance from Turkish and Balkan traditions. Its asymmetric meter creates a limping, energetic feel characteristic of Anatolian and Greek folk music.

#karsilama asRhythm to: #darbuka.
🥁

Sub-Saharan Africa

#shiko West Africa (Ghana/Nigeria)

A West African bell pattern — E(4,8) — used in the music of the Ewe people of Ghana and Togo. Toussaint documented it as one of the most widespread Euclidean rhythms in African traditional music. Often played on an iron bell (gankogui) as the timeline against which all other drums interlock.

#shiko asRhythm to: #bell.
#soukous Democratic Republic of Congo

The percussion pattern from Congolese soukous — a high-energy guitar-driven dance music that evolved from rumba and spread across Africa in the 1960s–80s. The rhythm supports the characteristic interlocking guitar lines and the animated kwassa kwassa dance style.

#soukous asRhythm to: #conga.
🎻

Europe

#tarantella Italy (Southern)

The driving 6/8 rhythm of the Southern Italian tarantella — a fast folk dance from Campania, Calabria, and Puglia. Historically associated with the tarantism trance ritual. Its relentless forward motion is produced by interlocking tambourine (tammorra) patterns at breakneck speed.

#tarantella asRhythm to: #tamb.
#buleria Spain (Flamenco)

The most complex and fastest compás in flamenco — a 12-beat cycle grouped as 3+3+2+2+2 with a characteristic accent on beats 3, 6, 8, 10, 12. The bulerías emerged in Jerez de la Frontera and is considered the madre (mother) of all flamenco palos due to its improvisational flexibility.

#buleria asRhythm to: #cajon.

Exploring in Pharo

The definitive list — including future additions — is always accessible inside your Pharo image. Evaluate in a Playground and open an Inspector: 

Rhythm list.   "CMD+I or CTRL+I to inspect"

Each method in the Rhythm class contains detailed ethnomusicological comments. Open the Class Browser, navigate to Rhythm, and read the comments alongside the binary pattern defined with Time Unit Box notation.

ℹ️ The patterns documented here are representative approximations. The actual binary arrays in the source code are the authoritative versions — verify in your image when precision matters.
